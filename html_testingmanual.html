Part 1: Manual
(test cases, bug reports)

User story, ep. 1: Enlightment

Lukasz has finally discovered the diverse world of IT which had always been there but Lukasz was too blind to see it. The more blind he had been, the more intense his experiencing of IT-environment is nowadays.

After having tried various aspects of the matter he finally realized that all his experiences, all his actions and all the decisions he had made led to this precious moment:

to the resolution of becoming a software tester.

User story, ep. 2: Theory

So he started where many university-graduates think they should start from - buying / borrowing / downloading tons of books. Some he read from cover to cover, some he left unfinished and some he abandoned after taking look at the contents.

Yet despite the efforts of the authors he couldn't truly comprehense what was being written without previous software testing experience whatsoever.

He needed some practice.

User story, ep. 3: More theory

There was one book however, the book of books, the bible, the almanach he inevitably encountered. The ISTQB syllabus.

A merely 100-pages volume to encompass the foundations of knowledge every software tester just can't do without. Definitely not a thrilling bedtime reading piece of art to be honest. But Lukasz continued turning the pages and getting familiar with the tech-vocabulary...

to find out that he has already been testing a lot!

User story, ep. 4: Exploratory & Acceptance tests

Whenever he was playing a video-game on his PC, searching for his favourite tv-series on Netflix or buying christmas gifts online he was actually running kind of manual exploratory (but strongly unorganized and informal) tests!

And he carried out some acceptance tests (both UAT & OAT) as well, when he was veryfying the functionalities of the sale & booking application that was about to be implemented at his work (more about that implementation here). This time he kept the tests as organized and formal as possible, learning how to create test documentation (tests cases, bug reports).

The thought he wasn't a total newbie after all was kind of refreshing.

User story, ep. 5: Test cases

So finally he was ready to run some real organized pre-planned manual testing. A TestLink software was exactly what he was looking for to manage the test cases he was about to invent.

Although not super-intuitive, not easy to install and not easy to set up, the tool soon made recording of the test cases really straightforward and enjoyable.

See below how it went:

Manual online bookstore test

Scenario: At first I examined the behaviour of an online bookstore searchbox. With no specification at hand all I could do was to validate whether the searchbox behaviour met my personal expectations. In other words: I made this test fail on purpose hoping the owners won't ever hear about this.

Environment:
Tool: Testlink (v. 1_0_20_fixed)
OS: Windows 10 Home 64-bit
Browser: Google chrome

User story, ep. 6: Bug reports

The time has come to report some bugs. Firstly Lukasz tested a chat app (for mobile devices) filled with bugs located all over the place for testing purposes. He wrote his report in a word document.

Secondly he learned, that there was a group of applications build for the purpose of testing education, called Mr Buggy, that served as tasks during actual championships in software testing. The tool he chose to report a 'Mr Buggy bug' was Jira - a proprietary (non-free) but still super-popular software that allows bug tracking and agile project management. Luckily there was a free trial version he could sing up for.

See below how it went:
